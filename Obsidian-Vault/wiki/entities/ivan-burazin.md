# Ivan Burazin

Ivan Burazin is the founder of [[entities/daytona|Daytona]], a secure and elastic infrastructure purposely built for running AI-generated code. He spoke at AI Engineer World's Fair 2025 on Agent Experience (AX) as the defining discipline for building tools in the agent-first era.

## Background

Burazin's career spans the full stack of infrastructure:

- **Early 2000s:** Built data centers and server rooms — HP servers, IBM racks, VMware hypervisors, physically screwing in servers and running cables. ^[extracted]
- **2009:** Created the very first browser-based IDE, predating Replit by years. Built custom orchestrators and isolation systems from scratch. ^[extracted]
- **Later:** Led developer experience at [[entities/infobip|Infobip]], a multi-billion dollar communications platform as a service (CPS) competing with Twilio — one API for emails, SMS, voice, and more. ^[extracted]
- **Recent:** Working at Daytona with people he's worked with historically, noting that founding companies with former colleagues makes the work "so much more fun." ^[extracted]

## AIEF2025 Talk

At AI Engineer World's Fair 2025, Burazin presented "AX is the only Experience that Matters," arguing that:

1. The number of AI agents will be humans to the power of n — already 25% of YC startups say AI writes 95% of their code, and 37% of the latest YC batch are building agents as their products
2. Most tools built today break the moment you remove a human from the loop
3. Agents will be the developers, not co-pilots — building dev tools for humans is building for the past
4. **Agent Experience (AX)** is the term for building tools for agents, coined by Matt Coates (co-founder of Netlify)
5. The critical missing word in current AX thinking is **autonomously** — agents must access, understand, and operate without human fallback
6. Daytona is what a laptop is to a human: a computing environment agents use to run code, do data analysis, reinforcement learning, and computer use

## Key Views on Agent Experience

Burazin argues that the industry's current approach to agent-facing tooling (APIs, readable docs, seamless auth) is necessary but insufficient. The word "autonomously" in Sean's definition of AX — "how easily can agents *autonomously* access, understand, and operate within digital environments" — is the differentiator. Tools that still require human fallback are "porting for the past." ^[extracted]
