# Greg Brockman

Greg Brockman is a co-founder of [[entities/openai|OpenAI]] and previously served as the first CTO of [[entities/stripe|Stripe]], joining when the company had 4 employees and growing it to 250. ^[extracted]

## Early Life and Inspiration

Brockman's path to programming was accidental. He originally aspired to be a mathematician, inspired by figures like [[concepts/first-principles-thinking|Galois and Gauss]] whose work endured for centuries. ^[extracted] During high school he was involved in theater, chemistry, and math. He wrote a calendar scheduling app that introduced him to coding. ^[extracted]

The pivotal moment came after high school when he wrote a chemistry textbook. A friend told him no one would publish it and suggested he make a website instead. Brockman went to [[entities/w3schools|W3Schools]] and learned PHP, building a table-sorting widget as his first project. ^[extracted] The moment he clicked a column header and it sorted exactly as he envisioned felt like "magic" to him. ^[extracted]

He came to view programming as fundamentally different from mathematics: in math you write a proof and three people care; in programming you write a program and everyone benefits from what was in your head without needing to understand the code. ^[extracted] This philosophy of building over theorizing shaped his entire career. ^[inferred]

## Stripe Era

Brockman was cold-emailed by Stripe while still in college through mutual friends at [[entities/harvard|Harvard]] and [[entities/mit|MIT]] — he had dropped out of Harvard to attend MIT at the time. ^[extracted] He met co-founder [[entities/patrick-collison|Patrick Collison] and felt an immediate connection: "this is the kind of person that I've wanted to work with and been looking for." ^[extracted]

He dropped out of MIT and flew to join Stripe as the first CTO, growing the team from 4 to 250 people. ^[extracted] One notable early episode involved integrating with [[entities/wells-fargo|Wells Fargo]]'s payment backend. The bank claimed the technical integration would take nine months; Brockman's team completed it in 24 hours by treating it like a college problem set — Brockman implementing everything, John testing from the top of the test script, and Dara working from the bottom up. ^[extracted]

## Philosophy on Speed and Constraints

Brockman's advice on working fast is nuanced: it's not about ignoring all constraints, but about mapping to where there is unnecessary overhead from constraints that no longer apply to your specific situation. ^[extracted] He frames this as thinking from first principles — finding where things genuinely need to be slow versus where they are slow only because of inherited organizational habits. ^[extracted] This philosophy became especially relevant in the AI era, where tools like [[entities/codex|Codex]] accelerate productivity. ^[inferred]

## Independent Study

Brockman has a long history of independent study, beginning in sixth grade when his father taught him algebra. In seventh grade he skipped pre-algebra and moved directly to eighth-grade math after proving himself in class. ^[extracted] He has repeated this pattern through [[entities/recur-center|Recur Center]] programs and sabbaticals, crediting self-directed learning as a key part of how he grew up and developed. ^[extracted]
