Los Alamos National Laboratory (LANL) is a U.S. Department of Energy national laboratory managed by [[entities/mark-myshatyn|Mark Myshatyn]]'s enterprise AI office. Founded after the Manhattan Project, LANL has been doing applied statistics and machine learning for nearly 70 years — dating back to 1956 when they ran "Los Alamos Chess" on the Maniac I supercomputer with no memory to hold an entire chessboard.

The laboratory spans 40 square miles with 13 nuclear facilities and approximately 20,000 researchers. Its mission includes nuclear stockpile stewardship, national security research, and pushing the boundaries of AI for science. LANL writes its own AI models rather than consuming only commercial or open-source tools, operating under the U.S. National Security AI Office's charge to "push the science of AI faster."

LANL handles data across multiple classification levels: open/public unrestricted data (like their ICF capsule design agent), controlled unclassified data, classified data, and DOE-restricted data. The physics of nuclear weapons never expires — it is born classified and stays classified forever, creating unique challenges for AI systems that must operate within these constraints.

Key partnerships include the UC family of schools for academic AI development, NVIDIA and HPE for the Venato supercomputer (2,500+ nodes of Grace Hopper superchips), and frontier labs like OpenAI for chem/bio safety work. LANL positions itself as "a safe place to do dangerous things," making it a trusted partner for frontier labs that cannot freely hire or share data.

LANL operates the Venato supercomputer, bringing frontier models like OpenAI's onto classified networks for mission-specific AI research.
