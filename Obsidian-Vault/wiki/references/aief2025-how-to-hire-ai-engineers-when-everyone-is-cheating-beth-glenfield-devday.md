# How to Hire AI Engineers when EVERYONE is cheating with AI — Beth Glenfield, DevDay

Beth Glenfield delivered this talk at DevDay (presented at the AI Engineer World's Fair 2025), arguing that AI is fundamentally breaking technical recruitment and that small companies are losing the talent war to Google and Meta not on compensation but on brand recognition, career prestige, and perceived stability. ^[extracted]

## Key Claims

**AI cheating in interviews is widespread:** A company called Cluey (founded by an ex-Columbia student, raised $5.3M, heading toward $1M ARR) offers AI cheating services for technical interviews. LeeCo Wizard reports 93% success rates for Google and Meta interviews. One in three interviews now involve AI assistants. ^[extracted]

**Traditional coding puzzles are obsolete:** Lead code puzzles no longer predict who will build great AI products. The candidates who actually want to work on AI — creative problem solvers, collaborative leaders, people who can work with AI — are not being optimized for lead code performance. Instead they're building AI tools, using AI libraries, contributing to open source, and understanding business impact. ^[extracted]

**Salesforce is stopping software engineer hiring:** Mark Benioff announced Salesforce will no longer hire software engineers this year, citing a 30% productivity boost since replacing people with AI. Glenfield noted she'd like to see the data on that claim. ^[extracted]

**Zuckerberg and TechCrunch predict AI displacement:** Mark Zuckerberg says AI will handle mid-level engineering work by later this year. TechCrunch reports entry-level engineering jobs are being wiped out — but these are different engineering jobs, not fewer ones. ^[extracted]

## DevDay's Proposed Hiring Process

DevDay has completely reimagined the technical hiring process with **real-life workplace simulations** where candidates work alongside AI agents with different personalities (perfectionist, pragmatist, security expert, junior developer needing mentoring). Candidates build features for the company's actual business domain rather than solving contrived problems. ^[extracted]

Instead of measuring coding ability, DevDay measures:
- How candidates collaborate with AI
- How they handle ambiguity
- How they communicate technical decisions in pull requests and ticket comments
- How they mentor others and adapt when requirements change mid-sprint ^[extracted]

Glenfield argues that only Google and Meta can brute-force their hiring (interview 100 candidates, hire 5, give great paychecks). For most companies, one bad hire costs $20k–$60k and hiring someone who doesn't know how to ship AI products is catastrophic. ^[extracted]
