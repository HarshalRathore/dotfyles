# AIEF2025 — Why your product needs an AI product manager, and why it should be you

**Speaker:** James Lowe, Head of AI Engineering, Incubator for AI (i.AI)  
**Event:** AI Engineer World's Fair 2025  
**Video:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xzJdSi2Tsqw

## Summary

James Lowe makes the case for the AI product manager as a critical role (or mindset) in building AI-powered products. Drawing on two case studies from the UK government's Incubator for AI — Consult/Theme Finder and Minute — he delivers three lessons: evaluate AI early, prioritize AI capability before product design, and explore features broadly when uncertain.

## Key Arguments

1. **AI product management has a bright future.** Andrew Ng's post: AI coding agents make prototyping cheaper, and AI features make the impossible possible, increasing demand for people who can decide what to build.

2. **AI complicates the traditional product management triangle.** It adds uncertainty to business viability (more experimentation needed), technology (how to evaluate AI?), and user experience (probabilistic outputs, guardrails, human-in-the-loop). At the center: is what you're doing even possible?

3. **AI product managers need data and AI proficiency.** Product people must upskill in AI; AI engineers have a good background for product management.

4. **It's a mindset, not a role.** Having someone grappling with business, technology, users, and AI possibility is what matters — not having a job title.

## Case Studies

### Consult / Theme Finder

Government consultations attract hundreds of thousands of responses. Analyzing them manually takes months and costs millions. i.AI's initial approach (BERTopic-based) produced inaccurate results. Pivoting to evaluate-first — real data, synthetic eval data, optimization, user testing — produced output comparable to humans but 1,000× faster and 400× cheaper. The evaluation process revealed where human-in-the-loop was most valuable, fundamentally changing the product.

### Minute

AI transcription for government meetings. i.AI explored many features using AI coding assistance, tested with different user groups, then stripped back to focus on what worked. AI coding meant no sentimental attachment to features — easy to cut what didn't work.

## Three Lessons

1. **Resolve AI uncertainties early** with evaluations and tests with real users
2. **Go wide with features** when uncertain, then strip back
3. **Combine product and engineering** into as few people as possible — few great things are created by committee
