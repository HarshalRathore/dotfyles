## Talk Summary

Raiza Martin, product leader at Huxe and former PM on NotebookLM at Google, delivered a talk at the AI Engineer World's Fair 2025 centered on the thesis that "everything is ugly, so go build something that isn't."

### Key Arguments

**Everything is at its ugliest right now.** Martin argues that every product in use today was created in the pre-AI era, where interactions were constrained by what was technically feasible. Products feel awkward because they were designed for a world where you press a button and a "J" appears on screen — not a world where you can describe what you want and get something magical back. She uses the analogy of a microwave: in an AI-native world, we might want an "AI microwave" that understands what we need rather than requiring us to learn its interface.

**The multi-layer cake model of product.** Martin frames product as a four-layer cake:
1. **Self** — understanding your own values and what you bring to the table
2. **Team** — teams are being drastically reconfigured with "invisible participants" (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) behind every document and slide
3. **Product** — pulling an idea from deep within yourself and translating it into technology
4. **User** — the layer where consumer unrest emerges

**Consumer unrest creates a chasm.** Users who experience AI-native products like ChatGPT, Cursor, and Claude — where "it's super easy to use, I just say what I want and something magical comes back" — then encounter legacy products and experience friction. This creates a growing gap between AI-native products and the rest of the software world.

**Everything gets rebuilt.** Martin predicts a phase where "everything gets rebuilt" to match the interaction richness that AI makes possible. This is not a gentle evolution; it's a complete rewrite of every layer.

**Personal clarity is the foundation.** Building a product that meaningfully exists in people's lives is a "forceful" experience. It requires personal clarity in three dimensions: vision, purpose, and taste. This clarity must start with a singular individual before it can propagate to teams and organizations.

**Product's job is to find opportunity and "explode it."** Martin compares the product person's role to a popcorn kernel: find the nugget of opportunity and explode it. This mission applies regardless of whether you call yourself a product manager, engineer, or designer — anyone who embodies the force behind a product shares this responsibility.

**Chaos and opportunity are complementary.** Every layer of product building is being rewritten, and this is "pretty fucking hard." Role uncertainty (will there still be PMs? engineers? designers?) creates discomfort. But complementary to chaos is always opportunity.

### Personal Context

Martin dropped out of college early in her career, then returned for full-time studies while simultaneously building NotebookLM at Google. This dual commitment gave her deep personal clarity about the product she wanted to create — a tool where you could put docs and slides in and chat with them to get something valuable. The road to shipping the first version (codenamed "Tailwind," announced at Google I/O 2023) was marked by significant skepticism from users, stakeholders, and teammates.
