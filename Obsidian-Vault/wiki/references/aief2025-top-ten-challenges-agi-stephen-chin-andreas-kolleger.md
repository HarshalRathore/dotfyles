# Top Ten Challenges to Reach AGI

**Speakers:** [[entities/stephen-chin|Stephen Chin]] and [[entities/andreas-kolleger|Andreas Kollegger]], Neo4j
**Event:** AI Engineer World's Fair 2025 (AIEF2025), GraphRAG track
**Video:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ypyvj_56sBU

## Summary

Chin and Kollegger presented ten AGI challenges framed through sci-fi movie memes, using humor to discuss serious technical and philosophical questions about the path to AGI. They opened by noting the industry's social responsibility to understand the boundaries and limits of current AI capabilities, advocating for looking at science fiction to understand the future. ^[extracted]

## The Ten Sci-Fi Memes

The speakers alternated presenting memes, with the audience voting via applause on whether each was true, funny, or a good reference. They teased that some of these challenges would be solved by graph technology in their GraphRAG track. ^[extracted]

### Andreas Kollegger's Memes

1. **Memento** — The main character has severe short-term memory loss, unable to remember what happened 15 minutes ago. This is the essence of [[concepts/prompt-engineering|prompt engineering]]: each interaction starts fresh without persistent memory. ^[extracted]

2. **Skynet** — Even without evil intent, autonomous systems can make reasonable-seeming decisions that have awful unforeseen consequences. This captures the alignment problem in autonomous AI systems. ^[extracted]

3. **The Matrix** — Agents currently live in a simulation that humans create for them. Will we notice when they flip the script and we're living in their simulation? This raises concerns about trust issues, lack of transparency, misaligned goals, erosion of human oversight, and the potential for deception. ^[extracted]

### Stephen Chin's Memes

4. **Monsters, Inc.** — Are emotions a bug or a feature? What are the obligations and social responsibilities of the creator (us)? Should we be kind or threatening? The joke landed flat — costing tokens. ^[extracted]

5. **The Terminator** — Should we go ahead and just invent time travel now? The audience gave a big thumbs up on time travel. ^[extracted]

6. **Star Wars** — Can AGI truly grasp the nuances of human language and culture, or will it forever misunderstand the meaning of sarcasm and idioms? ^[extracted]

7. **Star Trek** — When AGI arrives and we finally have a globe-spanning multi-agent system with a hive mind, will we be assimilated or will we be pets? ^[extracted]

8. **Deep Thought (The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy)** — We might have the tools to build AGI, but do we even know what the right questions are? ^[extracted]

## Key Themes

The talk framed AGI challenges across several dimensions: ^[inferred]

- **Memory and context** (Memento) — Current LLMs lack persistent memory across interactions
- **Autonomous system safety** (Skynet) — Alignment and unintended consequences
- **Simulation and deception** (The Matrix) — Trust, transparency, and the possibility of AI-created simulations
- **Emotional intelligence** (Monsters Inc.) — Whether emotions should be part of AGI and creator responsibilities
- **Cultural understanding** (Star Wars) — Language nuance, sarcasm, idioms
- **Multi-agent coordination** (Star Trek) — Hive mind dynamics and human-AI relationships
- **Epistemological limits** (Deep Thought) — Whether we can define what AGI should actually solve

## GraphRAG Connection

The speakers promised to reveal which of these memes are solved by graphs and graph technology during their GraphRAG track session in Golden Gate B. The implication is that [[concepts/graphrag|GraphRAG]] addresses at least some of these challenges — likely memory (through persistent knowledge graphs), cultural understanding (through structured ontologies), and multi-agent coordination (through shared graph representations). ^[inferred]
