# Useful General Intelligence — Danielle Perszyk, Amazon AGI

> **Source:** AI Engineer World's Fair 2025 | [[entities/danielle-perszyk|Danielle Perszyk]], Cognitive Scientist at [[entities/amazon-agi|Amazon AGI SF Lab]]
> **Video:** <https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Dj0b_cEBHHI>

## Summary

Perszyk proposes a fundamental reframing of AGI from building machines that are as smart or smarter than humans to building AI that makes humans *smarter*. She draws on cognitive science (the brain as prediction machine, perception as controlled hallucination), evolutionary anthropology (techno-social co-evolution, language as the origin of generalization), and computing history (Engelbart's augmentation vision) to argue that general intelligence is inherently social and distributed — emerging through interactions, not within isolated minds or models.

The concrete manifestation is [[entities/nova-act|Nova Act]], Amazon's computer-use agent SDK, which grounds agent interactions in a shared visual environment (the browser). Perszyk argues this grounding is necessary but not sufficient; agents will eventually need models of human minds to achieve true reliability.

## Key Claims

1. **Hallucinations are features, not bugs** — They allow going beyond the data, just as the brain's predictive processing enables flexible intelligence. The challenge is controlling them, not eliminating them. ^[extracted]

2. **General intelligence doesn't live in individuals** — It emerges through social interaction and distributed cognition. "Intelligence of the variety that humans have can't exist in a vacuum. It doesn't exist in individual humans. It won't exist in individual models. Instead, general intelligence emerges through our interactions." ^[extracted]

3. **Language co-evolved with models of mind** — Language is not just communication; it's the mechanism by which we reproduce the contents of our minds. Models of mind became "the first variable" — the origin of generalization. ^[extracted]

4. **The two paths for AI** — The Dartmouth path (make AI smarter) vs the Engelbart path (make humans smarter). Amazon AGI chooses Engelbart. ^[extracted]

5. **Automation does not guarantee augmentation** — Offloading cognitive work can free attention for expertise building, but it can also reduce agency (scrolling, echo chambers, auto-complete shutting down thinking). ^[extracted]

6. **Agents need models of our minds** — Reliability is not about clicking the same place every time; it's about understanding the larger goal. Agents will need theory of mind. ^[extracted]

7. **Reliability requires a shared environment** — Computer-use agents (seeing pixels, interacting with UIs) provide grounding that text-only LLM wrappers lack. ^[extracted]

8. **Human-agent interaction data is essential** — To advance agents with models of mind, we need data from real human-agent interactions. Useful products motivate usage, which generates data, which improves models. ^[extracted]

## Cognitive Technologies Sequence

Perszyk traces this historical progression, each serving as foundation for the next: ^[extracted]

1. **Language** (~6 million years ago) — The original cognitive technology; co-evolved with models of mind
2. **Writing** — Externalized language, persistent knowledge
3. **Mathematics** — Abstract symbolic reasoning
4. **Computers / Internet** — Engelbart's augmentation machines
5. **AI Agents** — The emerging cognitive technology

## Key Quotes

> "We don't want AI to replace us or replicate us. We want it to complement us. We want AI plus humans to be greater than the sum of our parts." — Danielle Perszyk ^[extracted]

> "History of upgrading our intelligence didn't start with computers. It started with an evolutionary adaptation that allowed us to use each other's minds as tools." — Danielle Perszyk ^[extracted]

> "The thing that makes our intelligence general and flexible is inferring the existence of other minds." — Danielle Perszyk ^[extracted]

> "Agents can become our collective subconscious." — Danielle Perszyk ^[extracted]
