# Structuring a modern AI team — Denys Linkov, Wisedocs

**Speaker:** Denys Linkov, ML Team Lead at [[entities/wisedocs|WiseDocs]]
**Event:** AI Engineer World's Fair 2025
**Date:** 2025-07-24
**Duration:** ~17 minutes
**Video:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SbUxRluVRwk

## Overview

Denys Linkov structures his talk around three themes: the anatomy of an AI team (starting with company types), the evolution and value of generalists, and practical questions about hiring for AI roles. The central thesis is that technology is rarely the bottleneck — how you use technology, and what kind of team you build to use it, is what matters. ^[extracted]

## Three Themes

### 1. Anatomy of an AI Team

Linkov begins by classifying companies on a spectrum:

- **Technology companies** — technology is the core value proposition (big tech, startups). Challenge: lack of domain knowledge when launching products, business-technology misalignment.
- **Verticalized solutions / services companies** — domain-specific AI products (WiseDocs, Palantir). Challenge: everything goes right or everything goes poorly.
- **Tech-enabled companies** — core product is not technology but benefits from good tech (banks, retailers, SMBs). Challenge: tech is not the core value proposition.

Each type makes different decisions: tech companies buy data/expertise, verticalized companies face binary outcomes, and tech-enabled companies buy technology from vendors or build in-house. ^[extracted]

### 2. The Evolution of a Generalist

Linkov shares his experience building the first ML team at a conversational AI platform. His approach: hire generalists, support them with automation, and build a custom MLOps platform. The team owned six microservices and achieved goals like serving hundreds of thousands of concurrent models, multi-domain support, cost efficiency, and real-time training/serving. ^[extracted]

He frames hiring decisions along three axes: model training, model serving, and business acumen. For each, he argues for a "good enough" bar rather than top-tier specialization. ^[extracted]

### 3. The Question of Hiring

Key hiring arguments:
- **AI researchers are not always needed** — pre-training/fine-tuning models is rarely the first step toward AI value
- **Domain knowledge beats pure AI talent** — his "Ampere's Wager" thought experiment challenges trading a domain-knowledgeable team for top AI researchers
- **Identify your bottleneck first** — before hiring, ask what's actually stopping success: shipping features, acquiring users, retention, monetization, scalability, or reliability ^[extracted]

## Key Quotes

- "We are now an AI-first company... before you hire a person, you need to make the claim that you can't hire an AI agent or use AI." ^[extracted]
- "I think we have 90% of the technology to solve the problems of humanity." ^[extracted]
- "It's not about technology, it's how we use technology." ^[extracted]
- "Do you trade your team that has domain knowledge for five AI researchers from top labs?" ^[extracted]

## Key Claims

- 90% of technology needed to solve humanity's problems already exists; adoption is the bottleneck ^[extracted]
- The fax market still exists and is growing despite being archaic technology ^[extracted]
- It took 40 years after personal computing and the internet for medical systems to become digital ^[extracted]
- In 2017, only 3% of US payments were contactless ^[extracted]
- Companies like Shopify, Duolingo, and Zapier have announced "AI-first" transformations ^[extracted]
