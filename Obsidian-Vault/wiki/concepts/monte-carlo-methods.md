Monte Carlo methods are a broad class of computational algorithms that rely on repeated random sampling to obtain numerical results. They were developed at [[entities/los-alamos-national-laboratory|Los Alamos National Laboratory]] during and after the Manhattan Project, making them one of the earliest applications of computational statistics — predating modern AI by decades and underpinning [[concepts/agentic-science|agentic science]] today.

Mark Machotton, Enterprise AI Architect at LANL, noted that Monte Carlo methods developed at Los Alamos in the late 1940s and 1950s are "still in use today" — nearly 80 years later. This represents a continuous thread of applied computational science that predates modern AI by decades.

Monte Carlo methods are foundational to many modern AI/ML techniques:

- **Bayesian inference** uses Monte Carlo sampling (MCMC) for posterior estimation.
- **Reinforcement learning** uses Monte Carlo methods for value estimation.
- **Uncertainty quantification** in scientific ML relies on Monte Carlo simulation.
- **Optimization** problems in physics and engineering use Monte Carlo approaches.

For [[entities/los-alamos-national-laboratory|Los Alamos National Laboratory]], Monte Carlo methods represent the computational foundation upon which modern agentic AI is being built. The lab's 70-year history of applied statistics and simulation provides the domain tools and mathematical frameworks that AI agents leverage when executing code on HPC assets.
