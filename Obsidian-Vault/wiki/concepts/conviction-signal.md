## The Full-Time Question

Do you need to leave your full-time job to raise VC? The answer is mixed and firm-dependent. ^[extracted] Many VCs are comfortable with founders who are working on their startup as a side project while employed at a reputable tech company. ^[extracted]

## Why Conviction Matters

Venture capital is about backing companies that can return funds. VCs want founders with deep conviction about what they are building. ^[extracted] One signal of conviction is leaving your job to go all-in on the startup. ^[extracted] It is never a bad thing to have made that leap when raising from VCs. ^[extracted]

## The Psychology of VC Investing

Understanding VC psychology is important for founders who are raising from VCs. VCs are looking for people who have tons of conviction because they are betting on companies that need to return the entire fund. Founding signals like going full-time help VCs differentiate between casual side projects and serious ventures. ^[inferred]

## The Nuance

The speakers share that their own fund raised pre-product with the co-founder still employed — demonstrating that it is possible to raise without going full-time first. ^[extracted] But they also acknowledge it made the process easier when everything was potential rather than after building a product that needed to "wow" investors. ^[extracted]

## Practical Takeaway

If you are raising from VCs, going full-time is a strong signal but not a strict requirement. If you are not ready to leave your job, many firms will still consider your startup — especially if you have a side project with demonstrable progress and a clear vision. ^[inferred]
