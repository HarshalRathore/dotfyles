# Firefighting Prioritization

**Firefighting prioritization** is a decision framework for when multiple crises demand attention simultaneously. The core metaphor: you have one fire truck in a town that's completely on fire — where do you start?

## The Framework

Eric Simons of Bolt.new uses this framework both for extreme startup situations (like scaling Bolt.new from $0.7M to $20M ARR with <20 people) and for general startup operations:

1. **Identify high-impact areas.** Not all fires are equal. Some burn buildings to the ground; others burn trash cans. Assess impact, not urgency.
2. **Protect infrastructure.** The systems that keep the company running (code, data, platform) take priority over individual incidents.
3. **Save key people.** The people who can put out the most fires are themselves on fire. Protect them first.
4. **Accept that some fires will burn.** This is not failure — it's reality. A small team cannot save everything.

## The Two Types of Fires

Startups face two categories of "everything is on fire" moments:
- **Good fires** — too many customers, too much demand, support overwhelmed
- **Bad fires** — broken systems, security incidents, data loss

Both require the same prioritization discipline. The difference is emotional: good fires feel like success, bad fires feel like failure. But both consume the same finite resource: team capacity.

## The 10% Principle

"Focusing on 10% of the things often gets you the lion's share of the result that actually matters." This is not just a prioritization tactic — it's a structural advantage of small teams. Larger teams create pressure to do everything; small teams force clarity.

## When to Let Fires Burn

Not every fire deserves the truck. Fires to let burn typically include:
- Nice-to-have features that users requested but aren't critical
- Internal processes that are imperfect but functional
- Low-impact bugs that don't affect core user flows
- "Perfect" versions of things that are already working

The discipline of saying "no" to good-enough fires is what makes firefighting prioritization work.
