# Dial-Up Era of AI

The argument that we are in the early, "dial-up" phase of the AI era — where raw capability exists but the transformative applications have not yet been built. Articulated by [[entities/sarah-guo|Sarah Guo]].

## The Analogy

"We're in the dial-up era of AI, and we're moving pretty quickly to broadband." ^[extracted]

Guo draws the parallel to the iPhone ecosystem: ^[extracted]

| Platform milestone | Transformative company | Time lag |
|---|---|---|
| iPhone launched (2007) | Instagram | 4 years |
| iPhone launched | Uber | 5 years |
| iPhone launched | DoorDash | 6 years |

The point: the truly transformative companies "weren't necessarily the first people to recognize the changes or the opportunities. It was those who reimagined the experiences." ^[extracted]

## What Makes This Era Different

Unlike previous platform shifts, the AI "game board keeps getting shaken up" — every 12 months brings a new model release, a new capability breakthrough, or a 10x cost reduction. Every time the fundamentals shift, there's "an opportunity to win again." ^[extracted]

This directly connects to the [[concepts/execution-as-moat|execution as moat]] thesis — because the underlying technology keeps changing, companies that execute well through each shift can repeatedly gain advantage. ^[inferred]

## Implications

- The trillion-dollar AI company hasn't been founded yet ^[inferred]
- First-mover advantage is overrated (consistent with the [[concepts/execution-as-moat|execution as moat]] thesis) ^[inferred]
- The opportunity is not in building better models but in reimagining experiences ^[extracted]
- Engineers who got the "magic first" (40% of AI usage is still coding) are the translators for the rest of the world ^[extracted]
