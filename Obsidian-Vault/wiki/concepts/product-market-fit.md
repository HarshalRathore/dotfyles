# Product-Market Fit

**Product-market fit (PMF)** is the state where a product satisfies strong market demand — users want it, use it, and will pay for it. Before PMF, startups are in discovery mode, trying to figure out what to build. After PMF, the challenge shifts to scaling.

## The Sales Pipeline Analogy

Eric Simons of Bolt.new (StackBlitz) describes PMF discovery as analogous to enterprise sales: if you want to close $1M in pipeline, you don't talk to three people — you talk to 100-200 at the top of your funnel, half take the next call, maybe 10 are warm leads, and you close 3.^{inferred}

The same logic applies to building products: you need to take as many "shots on goal" as possible. Each product idea, feature, or iteration is a shot — most miss, but enough hits compound into PMF.

## Key Implications

**Runway is critical.** You need to stick around long enough to find PMF. Lower burn rate = more time to iterate. One of StackBlitz's competitors in the IDE space was acquired two weeks before Bolt.new launched — they were on the same trajectory but ran out of runway first.

**Inefficiency is normal.** Most startups are "incredibly inefficient" before finding PMF. This is not a failure mode — it's the expected state. The key is surviving long enough for the shots to land.

**Same core tech, different positioning.** StackBlitz had been building the same core technology for seven years before Bolt.new found PMF. The technology wasn't the problem — the commercial offering and market timing were.
