# First Principles Thinking

First principles thinking, as articulated by [[entities/greg-brockman|Greg Brockman]], is not the naive approach of "just ignore all constraints and do the thing." ^[extracted] Instead, it is the disciplined practice of mapping to where things genuinely need to be slow or done a certain way versus where they are slow only because of inherited constraints that no longer apply to your specific situation. ^[extracted]

## The Nuanced View

Brockman frames it this way: the general principle of "don't worry about constraints" is "not 100% true." ^[extracted] Real constraints exist — the art is distinguishing between:

1. **Genuine constraints** — physical, legal, or technical limits that cannot be bypassed
2. **Inherited overhead** — processes and timelines established for different circumstances that persist through organizational inertia

The Wells Fargo integration at [[entities/stripe|Stripe]] exemplifies this distinction: the nine-month timeline was not a genuine constraint but an inherited one from how the bank normally operated with enterprise customers. ^[extracted]

## AI-Era Relevance

Brockman notes this philosophy becomes especially relevant in the accelerating AI era, where tools like [[entities/codex|Codex]] dramatically increase individual productivity and make it easier to bypass traditional constraints. ^[inferred] The question shifts from "can we do this faster?" to "what actually needs to be this slow?"
