## Cold Emails Do Get Read

VCs read cold emails. ^[extracted] While they are inundated with messages, it is also their job to hunt for deals — they are salespeople selling capital to entrepreneurs. ^[extracted] However, cold emails that get answered are usually prompted by some kind of warm signal. ^[extracted]

## What Makes a Cold Email Work

The best cold emails share these characteristics:

1. **Specific ask**: A clear, tangible request (e.g., "I'd love your take on our go-to-market strategy" or "Do you have 15–20 minutes to chat?")
2. **Warm signal**: Reference to prior interaction, shared connection, or content the investor has produced
3. **Tangibility**: The recipient knows exactly what to expect from the conversation and what they can contribute

A good example: "My co-founders and I are taking time off from this company to build this thing. We've built out an MVP and started beta testing. I'd love your take on our go-to-market. And if it feels right, any pointers to other investors?" ^[extracted]

## What Makes a Cold Email Fail

- Vague requests with no specific ask
- Trying to put the whole pitch in the email
- Long emails that try to communicate everything upfront

## The Core Principle

"The job of the email is not to make the sale. It's just to compel a response because then you get the opportunity to make the sale." ^[extracted] Give enough information to compel a reply, not enough to close the deal. ^[inferred]

## Leveraging Warm Signals

VCs who post content on LinkedIn (e.g., twice or three times a week on topics they are thinking about) create natural warm signals. Entrepreneurs who reach out with relevant ideas after seeing that content are almost always replied to. ^[extracted] Referencing a specific blog post or article the investor has written is an effective cold-email tactic. ^[inferred]
