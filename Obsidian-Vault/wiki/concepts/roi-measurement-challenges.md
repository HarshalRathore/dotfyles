# ROI Measurement Challenges

Measuring the return on investment (ROI) of AI initiatives has proven to be a particularly thorny problem for enterprises, with traditional impact metrics struggling to keep pace with the new reality of AI-driven transformation. ^[extracted]

## The Paradox

According to KPMG's Pulse Survey, a striking **78% of organizations** say ROI will become a bigger consideration in the coming year, yet **78% also say** that traditional impact metrics and measures are having a very hard time keeping up with the new reality. ^[extracted]

This double-majority creates a significant tension: CIOs and other investment leaders consistently report that the ways they have measured impact from previous technologies and initiatives are falling flat when applied to AI.

## Why Traditional Metrics Fail

The challenge stems from fundamental differences between AI and previous technology waves:

- **Non-linear impact**: AI can affect multiple dimensions simultaneously (time, quality, new capabilities, revenue)
- **Capability creation**: AI doesn't just optimize existing processes — it creates entirely new ones
- **Compounding effects**: AI investments often enable other AI investments, creating cascading returns
- **Qualitative shifts**: Decision quality, organizational learning, and strategic positioning are hard to quantify

## The Response: Self-Reported Use Case Studies

Recognizing the information gap, Superintelligent launched a study in late 2025 collecting self-reported ROI data from organizations. The study asked participants to report on their actual AI use cases and categorize impact across eight dimensions:

1. Time savings
2. Increased output
3. Improvement in quality
4. New capabilities
5. Improved decision making
6. Cost savings
7. Increased revenue
8. Risk reduction

This approach represents a pragmatic attempt to gather real-world data where systematic measurement frameworks have not yet caught up with the pace of AI adoption. ^[extracted]
