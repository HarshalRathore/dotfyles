## The Principle

"Ask for advice, get money." ^[extracted] People want to be part of something. The earlier you bring someone in, the more a part of it they become. ^[extracted]

## How It Works

Start reaching out to investors saying "I'm about to do X, I'd love to get your advice." ^[extracted] These conversations often lead to deeper engagement and eventually fundraising opportunities. By framing the ask as advice-seeking rather than fundraising, you lower the barrier to entry for the investor. ^[inferred]

## The Psychology

Bringing investors in early creates a sense of ownership and involvement. When an investor feels they are part of something from the beginning, they are more likely to invest when you formally raise. ^[inferred] This is a relationship-building strategy that predates formal fundraising processes.

## Practical Application

- Post content on LinkedIn about topics you are thinking about early
- Use that content as a signal that you are in a space of ideation and want to talk to smarter people
- Entrepreneurs who reach out with relevant ideas after you've posted content are almost always replied to
- Frame conversations as casual idea-sharing, not formal pitch meetings

## The Contrast

This approach is much easier than waiting until you have a polished product and then saying "I'm running a fundraising process now." ^[extracted] By that point, investors are in evaluation mode rather than relationship mode. ^[inferred]
