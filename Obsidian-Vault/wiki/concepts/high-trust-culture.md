# High-Trust Culture

**High-Trust Culture** is the operating principle that small AI teams require high trust, minimal bureaucracy, continuous discussions, and people who can move fast without being managed. ^[extracted]

## Core Principles

### High Trust

People must be "in it because they're building something together and not in it for other reasons like politics or personal advancement." ^[extracted]

### Minimal Bureaucracy

"Process is kind of the death of really fast collaboration and tight feedback loops." Small teams should minimize process and maximize direct communication. ^[extracted]

### Continuous Discussions

Instead of formal processes, decisions happen through ongoing conversations. If you feel like someone's going to need a lot of management, don't hire them. ^[extracted]

### Self-Direction

People who can move fast without being managed. The ability to look at a problem, figure out how to solve it, and execute. ^[extracted]

## Politics Are the Death of Small Teams

Vik Paruchuri explicitly states: ^[extracted]

> Politics are the death of small teams. We want people who only care about the work, the people around them, and customers.

Minimal ego is required — some ego to advance ideas, but not so much that you fight for them at the detriment of the company's health. ^[extracted]

## Compensation Philosophy

Datalab pays top-of-market salaries. Vik finds it "weird" that startups pay $150-200K when they've raised $20M. His thesis: hire fewer people with higher salaries and get more done. ^[extracted]

## Patient Hiring

The worst hires come from rushing to fill roles. The best hires come from saying "let me find the best person and hire them" even without an immediate role. This parallels the NBA/NFL drafting debate: best player available vs. drafting for fit. ^[extracted]

## Remote vs. In Person

Vik advocates working in person for small teams that need to move fast. Remote culture makes it harder to distinguish people who ship from people who talk about shipping. ^[extracted]
