The Maniac I was one of the first supercomputers, built at [[entities/los-alamos-national-laboratory|Los Alamos National Laboratory]] after the Manhattan Project. It was part of the EDVAC family of computers and represented the cutting edge of computational capability in the 1950s — the same era when [[concepts/monte-carlo-methods|Monte Carlo methods]] were first developed at the lab.

In 1956, LANL scientists demonstrated "Los Alamos Chess" on the Maniac I — a chess variant with no bishops on the board. This demonstration is historically significant because the computer had so little memory that it could not hold an entire standard chessboard at once. Despite this severe hardware limitation, the scientists implemented a working chess program.

Mark Machotton, Enterprise AI Architect at LANL, cited this demonstration as evidence that LANL has been doing applied AI and machine learning for nearly 70 years — long before modern AI existed as a field. The Maniac I era represents the beginning of a continuous tradition of computational science at Los Alamos that extends through Monte Carlo methods, through decades of simulation work, to modern agentic AI systems running on the Venato supercomputer.
