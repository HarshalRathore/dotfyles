# Take Shots on Goal

**Take shots on goal** is the startup principle that finding product-market fit requires taking as many product iterations, experiments, and pivots as possible before running out of resources. The metaphor comes from sports: you don't score by taking one shot — you score by taking many.

## The Sales Pipeline Metaphor

Eric Simons compares PMF discovery to enterprise sales: to close $1M in pipeline, a salesperson doesn't talk to three people — they talk to 100-200 at the top of the funnel, half take the next call, maybe 10 are warm leads, and they close 3.^{inferred}

Product discovery works the same way. Each product idea, feature, or iteration is a shot. Most miss. But enough hits compound into product-market fit.

## The Runway Connection

The number of shots you can take is directly determined by your runway:
- **Lower burn rate** = more runway = more shots on goal
- **Humans are the most expensive line item** = headcount is the primary driver of burn
- **Staying around longer** = more opportunity to hit PMF

StackBlitz's competitor was acquired two weeks before Bolt.new launched — they were on the same trajectory but ran out of runway first. They literally couldn't take enough shots.

## The Bolt.new Example

StackBlitz took shots for seven years before hitting one that stuck with Bolt.new. Seven years of iteration, pivoting, and learning — the same core technology, different commercial offerings — until the market said "yes." The seven years were not wasted; they were the shots that preceded the hit.

## Implications

1. **Don't optimize for speed, optimize for duration.** Staying around longer matters more than moving fast.
2. **Every shot teaches something.** Even missed shots provide market signals.
3. **The competitor who runs out of runway first loses.** Quality doesn't matter if you're not there to compete.
4. **Small teams can take more shots.** Lower burn = longer runway = more opportunities.
