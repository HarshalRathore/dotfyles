# AI-Era Talent Competition

The AI-era talent competition describes how the hiring landscape has fundamentally shifted: companies no longer compete for AI engineers solely on compensation, but on brand recognition, career prestige, and perceived stability. ^[extracted]

## The New Competitive Dimensions

When a candidate chooses between a Series A startup and Google's AI division, most will choose Google — not because of pay, but because of security. This is amplified by the current macro environment: many layoffs are happening, and candidates are looking for stability. ^[extracted]

The dimensions that matter for attracting AI talent:

1. **Brand recognition** — candidates gravitate toward known entities
2. **Career prestige** — working on AI at a major company carries status
3. **Perceived stability** — in a layoff-heavy environment, security matters more than equity upside ^[extracted]

## Small Companies Are Getting Crushed

Beth Glenfield's framing: "small companies are getting crushed in the talent war." Only Google and Meta can brute-force their hiring (interview 100 candidates, hire 5, give great paychecks). For everyone else, one bad hire costs $20k–$60k and they can't afford to hire someone who doesn't know how to ship AI products. ^[extracted]

## The AI-Native Candidate

The candidates that companies actually want for AI development are not the ones optimized for lead code performance. They're the ones who:

- Build AI tools
- Use AI libraries
- Contribute to open source
- Understand business impact ^[extracted]

These traits are not measured by traditional coding interviews, creating a mismatch between what companies evaluate and what they actually need. ^[inferred]
