## Side Projects Are Viable Starting Points

Many VC firms are comfortable with founders who are employed at reputable tech companies and building their startup as a side project. ^[extracted] The key is having demonstrable progress: "I'm at a reputable tech company, I'm not ready to make the jump, but I've been working on this as a side project, here's what I've done." ^[extracted]

## The Transition Signal

While side projects are viable starting points, transitioning to full-time becomes a strong signal of conviction when approaching VCs. ^[extracted] The side-project phase is about building momentum and demonstrating progress; the full-time leap is about signaling commitment. ^[inferred]

## Real-World Example

The speakers' own fund raised pre-product with their co-founder still employed at his day job. ^[extracted] Their conversations with VCs were framed as "we're about to do X" — everything was potential, not demonstrated traction. ^[extracted] They found it easier to raise when everything was potential rather than after investing significant time in building a product that needed to impress investors. ^[extracted]

## Strategic Implication

Founders should not wait until they have left their job or built a complete product before starting fundraising conversations. Early engagement with investors — even when you are still employed — can create relationships that mature into funding when you are ready to go all-in. ^[inferred]
