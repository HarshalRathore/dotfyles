# Runway and Burn Rate

**Runway** is the amount of time a startup can operate before running out of money. **Burn rate** is the rate at which a startup spends its capital. The relationship is simple: runway = cash / burn rate.

## The Core Insight

Eric Simons of Bolt.new articulates a fundamental startup principle: the name of the game when finding product-market fit is taking as many shots on goal as possible. Since humans are the most expensive line item for any company, lower headcount = lower burn rate = more runway = more time to find PMF.

## The Competitor Example

One of StackBlitz's main competitors in the IDE space was acquired and "stripped for parts" two weeks before Bolt.new launched. That competitor was on the same trajectory — they would have been meaningful competitors — but they didn't have enough runway to reach the other side of the PMF search.^{inferred}

This illustrates the existential importance of runway: finding product-market fit is not just about building the right thing, it's about surviving long enough to find it.

## Bootstrapping as Runway Education

Simons and his co-founder learned runway discipline through bootstrapping a previous company from zero — being broke, living on couches, and learning "how far a dollar can stretch." This experience shaped StackBlitz's operating philosophy:

- **Humans are the most expensive thing** — hire carefully, keep the team small
- **Inefficiency is the default** — most startups burn too much finding PMF
- **Lower burn = more options** — every extra month of runway is another shot on goal

## Implications

- **Lean is not a cost-cutting measure, it's a survival strategy.** Small teams aren't just cheaper — they buy time.
- **Growth without runway is dangerous.** Rapid hiring before PMF creates a death spiral: more burn → less runway → pressure to ship before ready → worse product.
- **The competitor who runs out of runway first loses**, regardless of product quality.
