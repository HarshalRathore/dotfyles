# Emergent Intelligence

**Emergent intelligence** is the principle that intelligence arises not from individual components but from the interactions between autonomous units pursuing their own interests. This pattern appears across biological, economic, and social systems. ^[extracted]

## The Pattern

Jan Čern illustrates emergent intelligence through three analogies:

1. **Biological systems:** Individual neurons are simple cells. Over time, they grow axons and dendrites, establish connections, and learn to communicate to pursue their interests (getting nutrients). The collective behavior of many neurons is what we call intelligence. ^[extracted]

2. **Markets:** Individual market participants pursue their own interests. Their mutual interaction — trading, communicating, establishing new relationships — produces collective intelligence that is greater than the sum of its parts. Markets "think" in ways no individual participant can. ^[extracted]

3. **Companies:** Employees pursue their own interests within a company. Through interaction (e.g., Slack communication), the collective output of the company can exceed the intelligence of any individual employee. ^[extracted]

## Application to Computing

Čern argues that general intelligence in computing systems will emerge through the same pattern: not from ever-larger single models exhibiting super-intelligent behavior, but from the interaction of multiple entities (agents) pursuing their own goals. The collective behavior of these interacting agents will produce what we call general intelligence. ^[extracted]

## Requirements for Emergent Intelligence in Computing

1. **Autonomous units:** Agents must have their own goals and interests
2. **Communication:** Agents must be able to communicate (MCP provides this fabric)
3. **Interaction:** Agents must be able to establish relationships and transact
4. **Discovery:** Agents must be able to find new counterparts to interact with ^[inferred]
