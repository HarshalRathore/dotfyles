The economic case for robotic kitchen automation centers on labor cost, reliability, and consistency advantages over human chefs.

CloudChef's robot labor is described at approximately $12/hour, with the following advantages over human labor:

- **Always shows up** — no absenteeism, no sick days
- **No overtime** — predictable labor costs
- **No turnover** — no hiring/training costs
- **Novel kitchen deployment** — plugs into any arbitrary kitchen without pre-programming, learning ingredient locations and appliance configurations on the fly

In contrast, expert human chefs earn "$150,000 a year" and are subject to the typical problems of human labor: inconsistency, turnover, scheduling issues, and the need for training in each new kitchen environment.

Abraham's framing is that wheeled robots with two arms are "now way cheaper than human labor" for cooking tasks, and that the missing piece was software — not hardware. Once the software was solved ("put it through culinary school"), the economics work in favor of robots.

The broader economic implication is that commercial kitchen labor — traditionally one of the most expensive and inconsistent inputs for food businesses — becomes a predictable, low-cost utility. This aligns with CloudChef's mission: "make high-quality, nutritious food affordable to everyone" by automating "all commercial kitchen labor."
