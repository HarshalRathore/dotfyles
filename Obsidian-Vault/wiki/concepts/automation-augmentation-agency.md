# Automation → Augmentation → Agency

[[entities/danielle-perszyk|Danielle Perszyk]] (Amazon AGI) proposes a three-step framework for how tools affect human intelligence: automation can lead to augmentation which can lead to increased agency — but this chain is not automatic. ^[extracted]

## The Positive Path

1. **Automation** — Offloading cognitive work to tools frees attention for higher-order thinking.
2. **Augmentation** — Freed attention enables deliberate practice and skill development. "This is how we become expert at things. We start by paying conscious attention to the details, we practice, and then our brain moves things over to our subconscious. Automation frees up our attention to focus on other things." ^[extracted]
3. **Agency** — With augmented capabilities, humans gain greater control over their environment and decisions.

## The Failure Mode

Automation does not automatically lead to augmentation. Perszyk highlights several failure modes: ^[extracted]

> "The problem is that automation doesn't always lead to augmentation. Sometimes it even comes at a cost. How many hours have we lost to scrolling? Or how many echo chambers have we been trapped within? How many times has auto-complete just shut down our thinking?" ^[extracted]

When automation reduces cognitive engagement rather than freeing it, it reduces human agency rather than increasing it. This is the risk of increasingly intelligent agents: they might "cause more problems than they solve" if not designed with precise control. ^[extracted]

## The Design Challenge

Perszyk argues the outcome depends on having "precise control" and actively tailoring systems "the way that we want." The crossroads for AI development is between continuing to make AI smarter (focusing on un-hobbling the model) versus building AI that increases human agency (un-hobbling humans). ^[extracted]
