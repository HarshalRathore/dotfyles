# Building Over Theorizing

"Building over theorizing" is the philosophy that creating tangible things in the world has more immediate value than abstract intellectual work, even if the abstract work endures longer. ^[extracted]

## Greg Brockman's Formulation

[[entities/greg-brockman|Greg Brockman]] articulated this distinction while explaining why he chose programming over mathematics: ^[extracted]

> In math, you think hard about a problem, you understand it, you write it down in an obscure way you call it a proof, and then like three people will ever care. In programming, you write it down in an obscure way we call it a program, and then maybe only three people ever read that program and care about the code, but everyone gets the benefit. No one has to understand the details — that thing that was in your head, it's real, it's in the world. ^[extracted]

This framing helped Brockman abandon his aspiration to be a mathematician working on "100, 200, 300-year time horizon" problems in favor of becoming a builder. ^[extracted] He explicitly rejected the long time horizon: "Forget about that 100-year time horizon, I just want to build." ^[extracted]

## Implications for Engineering Culture

This philosophy prioritizes:
- **Tangible output** over abstract correctness
- **Broad accessibility** of results over deep understanding
- **Speed of creation** over perfection of design

It aligns with the [[concepts/first-principles-thinking|first principles]] mindset of stripping away inherited constraints — both emphasize doing rather than planning, shipping rather than theorizing. ^[inferred]
