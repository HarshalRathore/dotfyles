# Human Baseline Benchmarking

Human baseline benchmarking is a methodology for measuring AI intelligence that uses humans as the reference point. The approach, advocated by the [[entities/ark-prize-foundation|ARC Prize Foundation]], rests on the premise that humans are "the one proof point of general intelligence that we know about." ^[extracted]

## Core Principles

1. **Humans as the target** — Since humans are the only known example of general intelligence, they serve as the natural comparison point for AI systems. ^[extracted]
2. **Create a measurable gap** — Design problems that are feasible for humans but hard for AI. The gap between human and AI performance provides a quantifiable measure of progress toward AGI. ^[extracted]
3. **Guide research** — The gap identifies a specific class of problems that researchers need to investigate, creating a North Star for research direction. ^[extracted]

## Methodology

The ARC Prize Foundation conducts in-person human testing under controlled conditions:

- **400+ humans tested** in San Diego, with rented computers and strict data privacy protocols. ^[extracted]
- **Every task validated** as solvable by at least one human before inclusion in the benchmark. ^[extracted]
- **Individual task difficulty measured** — some tasks are trivial for humans while others take significant time, creating a detailed difficulty profile. ^[extracted]
- **First-party data collection** ensures data integrity and prevents contamination. ^[extracted]

## Relationship to AGI Claims

Kamradt asserts that as long as researchers can create problems that humans can do but AI cannot, AGI has not been achieved. The AGI threshold is crossed when AI can complete novel interactive tasks faster and more efficiently than humans. ^[extracted]
