# The Bitter Design Lesson

The Bitter Design Lesson, articulated by [[entities/maximillian-piras|Maximillian Piras]] at AI Engineer World's Fair 2025, applies [[entities/rich-sutton|Rich Sutton]]'s Bitter Lesson to product design. The argument: as long as scaling laws hold and each new model generation delivers meaningful capability improvements, the basis of competition is inference performance — not interface innovation. ^[extracted]

## The Proposition

Sutton's Bitter Lesson states that general methods that leverage computation ultimately outperform human-engineered approaches. Piras extends this to UX: as long as the next model matters more than the last, the interface is a commodity that must adapt to the model, not the other way around. ^[inferred]

Said plainly: if the model is not commoditized, the interface is the commodity. The primary job of every AI interface is conforming to the next model. ^[extracted]

## The ROI Case

This lesson is "bitter" because it produces uninspiring, converged [[concepts/bitter-layout|layouts]] — but the ROI is hard to argue against: adding one model to a dropdown makes the application Nx more intelligent. Until models overshoot user needs and commoditize, this is the rational design decision. ^[extracted]

## When It Changes

The Bitter Design Lesson applies until models commoditize — when the marginal model capability improvement no longer matters for most user needs. At that point, differentiation shifts back to interface design, interaction paradigms, and integration. Piras identifies this as the moment to explore the [[concepts/gardening-as-design-paradigm|gardening paradigm]] for AI UX. ^[inferred]
