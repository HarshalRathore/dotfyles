## The Pre-Revenue Thesis

You do not need revenue to raise VC capital. ^[extracted] Pre-seed and seed-stage VC funds are well-versed in investing in companies that are pre-revenue and sometimes pre-product. ^[extracted] These investors are backing the founder, the team they can recruit, and the vision for what the company can become — not current financial metrics. ^[extracted]

Different types of VC funds have different requirements. Later-stage and multi-stage funds typically look for ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue) growth or ARR itself. But pre-seed and seed funds are structured to invest earlier in the company lifecycle. ^[extracted]

## The Product Question

You also do not need a product to raise VC capital. ^[extracted] The speakers emphasize that investors at the earliest stages understand the product will look dramatically different in 7–10 years than what is pitched on day one. ^[extracted] Over-emphasizing a "perfect" product on day one is a mistake because the likelihood of that initial product being the final, sustainable version is low. ^[extracted]

Instead of obsessing over a polished product, founders should focus on: their own abilities, the team they can build around them, and their unique insight into the market. ^[inferred]

## Practical Implications

Founders should raise as early as possible — specifically, when they need to scale. ^[extracted] Going to market early, even when everything is potential, can be easier than waiting to build a "wow" product that impresses investors. ^[extracted] The earlier you bring investors into the conversation, the more invested they become in your success. ^[inferred]
