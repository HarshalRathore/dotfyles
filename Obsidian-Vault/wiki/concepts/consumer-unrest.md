## Consumer Unrest

Consumer Unrest describes the growing friction users experience when they transition from AI-native products to legacy software. It is the outermost layer of Raiza Martin's Product Multi-Layer Cake framework.

### The Mechanism

Users who experience AI-native products like ChatGPT, Cursor, and Claude develop a new baseline for interaction: "it's super easy to use, I just say what I want and something magical comes back." When they then encounter legacy products — software built in the pre-AI era — the contrast is jarring. Legacy products feel "dumb" and "janky" because they require users to learn interfaces, press buttons, and navigate workflows that no longer make sense in an AI-rich world.

### The Chasm

Martin describes this as a "chasm" between two worlds:
- **AI-native products**: Super powerful, intuitive, smart
- **The rest of the world**: Janky, awkward, built for a different era

This chasm is growing as more users experience AI-native tools. The unrest is both external (users complaining about legacy products) and internal (product builders feeling uncomfortable about the products they ship because they know better interactions are possible).

### The Rebuilding Wave

Consumer unrest is the market signal that drives the AI Product Rebuilding Wave — the prediction that "everything gets rebuilt" to match the interaction richness that AI makes possible. Products that fail to rebuild will face increasing user resistance.
