# E-commerce Evolution

The evolution of the store follows distinct eras defined by what gets digitized and at what scale. ^[extracted]

## Era 1: Traditional Store (100+ years ago)

Inventory was in the back of a shop. Customers had to talk to a clerk, tell them what they wanted, and the clerk fetched it. No browsing was possible. ^[extracted]

## Era 2: Big-Box Retail (1950s-60s)

Information systems enabled scaling the concept of a store. Inventory moved from the back to the front, enabling browsing. Big-box retailers like [[entities/walmart|Walmart]] and [[entities/home-depot|Home Depot]] emerged. ^[extracted]

## Era 3: Online E-commerce

The internet put the big-box store online, matching the scale of merchandise with the scale of distribution. Shoppers can browse anywhere in the world 24 hours a day. Walmart had almost 500 million online shoppers last month; Home Depot had 170 million. ^[extracted]

## Trade-off: Sea of Sameness

The sacrifice of online e-commerce was a "sea of sameness" — it became difficult to distinguish between similar products from different brands (e.g., Adidas, Reebok, Brooks). ^[extracted]

## Era 4: Agentic Commerce (emerging)

AI digitizes the participants and their interactions. The transition moves from static websites to merchant agents, from consumer browsing to consumer agents, and from low-level payment infrastructure to higher-level intent infrastructure. ^[extracted]

## Definition of a Store

Through this evolution, a store is defined as "a location for and a protocol that facilitates transactions" — merchants wanting to sell, buyers wanting to buy, and a system to mediate. ^[extracted]
