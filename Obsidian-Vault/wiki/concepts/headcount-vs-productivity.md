# Headcount vs Productivity

**Headcount vs Productivity** is the principle that adding people to a team does not linearly increase productivity — and can significantly decrease it through bureaucracy, meeting overload, and specialist silos. ^[extracted]

## The Silicon Valley Myth

The persistent Silicon Valley notion: raise money, hire a bunch of people, build more. Vik Paruchuri argues this "almost never works out perfectly." ^[extracted]

## Evidence: The DataQuest Layoffs

At [[entities/dataquest|DataQuest]], Vik observed that productivity and happiness actually **increased** after two rounds of layoffs (30→15→7). This was the empirical basis for the thesis. ^[extracted]

Specifically, a 3-person team cut to 1 person became more productive because it freed up the senior person's time from managing others. ^[extracted]

## Four Hypotheses for Why Smaller Teams Are More Productive

### 1. Specialist Silos

As companies scale, they hire specialists who can't flex across the company to solve key issues. Each specialist operates in a narrow box. ^[extracted]

### 2. Remote Process Overhead

Remote teams require intentional process and heavy syncing that eats into productive time. This leads to meeting overload, especially with middle management whose job is "professionally to manage." ^[extracted]

### 3. Senior Management Burden

Senior people get tied down managing junior people. The senior person's time — the most valuable — is consumed by coordination rather than building. ^[extracted]

### 4. Bureaucracy and Unclear Priorities

As companies grow, they build sinks, unclear priorities, and slow feedback loops. A friend at Amazon worked there for two years building a shopping cart button. ^[extracted]

The "golden period" comparison extends to the famous Hadoop-versus-shell-script lesson: a 64-core machine can replace an entire Hadoop cluster. The principle applies to teams too — complexity doesn't scale productivity. ^[extracted]

## The Inverse Relationship

The implication: reducing team size can **increase** productivity by:

- Eliminating specialist silos
- Reducing meeting overhead
- Freeing senior people's time
- Clarifying priorities (tiny teams must ruthlessly prioritize)
- Tightening feedback loops
