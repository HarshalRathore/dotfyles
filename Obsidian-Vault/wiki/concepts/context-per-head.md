# Context Per Head

**Context per head** is the principle that small teams where each person carries more institutional and domain context outperform larger, more distributed teams. The metric is not just "how much does each person know" but "how fast can each person act on what they know."

## The StackBlitz Mantra

Eric Simons of Bolt.new describes this as his company's decade-old mantra: "a small number of people with more context per head." The mechanism is straightforward:

1. **Agency without permission chains.** When you have deep context, you don't need to ask — you can just build. There's no chain of command to go through.
2. **Immediate impact.** People who understand the full system can make changes that ripple across the product without coordination overhead.
3. **Speed compounds.** Small teams with high context make decisions and execute faster, which matters enormously during extreme scale events (like growing from $0.7M to $20M ARR in 60 days).

## Why It Matters

The contrast with larger organizations is stark. When you hire more people, you create pressure to do everything — every new hire expects work, and the team feels obligated to give it. A small team with high context forces clearer thinking about what actually matters: "focusing on 10% of the things often gets you the lion's share of the result."

## Preconditions

High context per head requires:
- **Shared history** — StackBlitz's team had been working together for seven years before Bolt.new launched
- **Shared values** — low ego, high trust, obsessed with user success
- **Grit and resilience** — the foundation underneath operational chaos
- **Deep alignment** — not just on goals but on how to work together

Without these preconditions, small teams fracture under pressure. The "300-moment" (30-40K users, <20 people, no support staff) would have been impossible without seven years of shared experience.
