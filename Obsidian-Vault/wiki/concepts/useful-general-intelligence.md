# Useful General Intelligence

Useful General Intelligence (UGI) is a framing proposed by [[entities/danielle-perszyk|Danielle Perszyk]] (Amazon AGI) that reframes the goal of AI from building machines that are smarter than humans to building AI that makes humans smarter. The core insight is that general intelligence in humans is inherently social and distributed — it emerges through interactions, not within isolated individuals or models. Therefore, the measure of an AI's "intelligence" should be its effect on human intelligence, not standalone capability benchmarks. ^[extracted]

## Two Historical Threads

Perszyk contrasts two visions for AI that have coexisted since the 1950s: ^[extracted]

1. **Dartmouth thread (solving intelligence)** — Build thinking machines that replicate or exceed human intelligence. This is the standard AGI narrative, focused on making AI smarter and giving it more agency.
2. **[[entities/douglas-engelbart|Engelbart]] thread (augmenting intelligence)** — Build tools that make humans smarter. Engelbart invented the computer mouse and GUI not to replace human thinking but to augment it.

The UGI framing adopts Engelbart's perspective: "We don't want AI to replace us or replicate us. We want it to complement us. We want AI plus humans to be greater than the sum of our parts." ^[extracted]

## Un-Hobbling Humans

While the current trend in AI focuses on "un-hobbling the AI" — giving models more agency, longer context, better tool use — Perszyk argues this focus does not guarantee usefulness to humans. Instead, Amazon AGI's vision is to **un-hobble humans**: build AI that gives people more agency and makes them smarter. ^[extracted]

## The Road Not Taken

> "We can continue to make AI smarter and give it more agency. We can focus on un-hobbling the AI, as it's fashionable to say. But this doesn't guarantee that it will be useful to us." ^[extracted]

The UGI framing suggests that measuring model capabilities alone is insufficient — we must measure human outcomes: creativity, productivity, strategic thinking, and even states of flow. ^[inferred]
