# Hallucination Detection

**Hallucination Detection** is the practice of identifying when AI systems generate factually incorrect, fabricated, or unsupported information. It is a core evaluation metric for agentic systems, where the cost of hallucination can be high — from legal advice to financial data queries.

## The Hallucination Problem

Bennett opens his AIEF2025 talk with a vivid example: the Chicago Sun Times published a summer reading list generated by an AI contractor, which hallucinated an entire book — "The Last Algorithm" by Andy Weir (who had not written such a book). Worse examples include lawyers citing false case law (Butler Snow cited fabricated precedent defending the Alabama prison system) and Air Canada's chatbot promising refunds that didn't exist. ^[extracted]

These are not edge cases — they happen daily, and the problem compounds in agentic systems where one hallucinated intermediate result can cascade through multiple tool calls and decisions. ^[extracted]

## Measuring Hallucination

Hallucination detection is measured as a per-span evaluation metric in observability-driven development:

- **Ground truth comparison** — does the output match known facts?
- **RAG grounding check** — is the output supported by retrieved documents?
- **Internal consistency** — does the output contradict itself or earlier statements?
- **LLM-as-judge scoring** — a better LLM evaluates whether the output is grounded ^[extracted]

## Why It Matters for Agents

In a multi-step agentic workflow, hallucination can occur at any span:
- The LLM hallucinates the correct tool to call
- The LLM hallucinates parameters for a tool call
- The LLM hallucinates an interpretation of retrieved data
- The LLM hallucinates a final answer not supported by any retrieved data

Each of these requires detection at the point of occurrence, not just at the final output. ^[inferred]
