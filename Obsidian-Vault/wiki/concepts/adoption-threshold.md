# Adoption Threshold

The **Adoption Threshold** is the observation that there is a **10x difference** between an engineering organization where 90% of engineers use AI versus one where 100% use AI. Partial AI adoption creates a drag on the entire organization.

## The Binary Nature of AI Adoption

Dan Shipper's key insight from building [[entities/every-company|Every]]:

> If even 10% of your company is using a more traditional engineering method, you sort of have to lean all the way back over into that world.

This means that partial AI adoption doesn't give you a partial benefit — it actively slows down the AI-using engineers because the organization must accommodate traditional workflows (typing into code editors, manual code review processes, etc.).

## Why Partial Adoption Underperforms

The mechanism is organizational friction:

- When some engineers type into code editors and others work with AI agents, the organization must maintain both workflows
- Traditional engineering methods (code editors, manual processes) are slower, so the whole org "leans back" into that slower paradigm
- The AI-using engineers are prevented from doing the things that would be possible with 100% adoption
- The result: an org at 90% adoption is significantly worse than one at 100%, not just marginally better than 0%

## Implications

### All-or-Nothing Strategy

This creates an all-or-nothing adoption strategy. Rather than incrementally introducing AI tools, organizations should aim for 100% adoption as quickly as possible. The marginal benefit of going from 90% to 100% is far larger than the marginal benefit of going from 0% to 10%.

### Cultural Change Required

Achieving 100% adoption requires more than tool procurement — it requires organizational change. Engineers who are accustomed to traditional workflows must transition, and the organization must be structured to support AI-native workflows exclusively.

### Connection to Compounding Engineering

The adoption threshold is closely related to [[concepts/compounding-engineering|compounding engineering]]. The compounding loop (plan, delegate, assess, codify) only works effectively when the entire organization participates. If only some engineers are in the loop, the codification step produces artifacts that the rest of the organization doesn't use, breaking the compounding effect.
