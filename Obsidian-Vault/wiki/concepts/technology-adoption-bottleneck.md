# Technology Adoption Bottleneck

The observation that technology capability is rarely the limiting factor for organizational success — adoption, usage, and change management are the real bottlenecks. Articulated by [[entities/denys-linkov|Denys Linkov]] at AIEF2025 with the provocative claim that "we have 90% of the technology to solve the problems of humanity." ^[extracted]

## The Core Thesis

Technology is not the thing stopping organizations from achieving success. The limitation is **how we use technology** — adoption, implementation, and organizational change. ^[extracted]

## Evidence: Archaic Systems Persist

Linkov provides several data points showing that archaic technology persists despite modern alternatives:

- **The fax market still exists** — and is growing, with billions of dollars spent on it
- **Contactless payments** — in 2017, only 3% of payments in the US were contactless (higher now, but still lagging)
- **Checks remain massive** — despite digital payment options, checks are still a major part of the market
- **Electronic medical records** — it took 40 years after personal computing and the internet for medical systems to become digital (higher now, but the lag is telling)

These examples illustrate a pattern: technology adoption is slow, incomplete, and often resists available solutions. ^[extracted]

## Implications for AI Teams

If technology is not the bottleneck, then AI teams should focus on:

1. **Adoption strategy** — How to get people to use AI tools effectively
2. **Change management** — Overcoming organizational resistance to new technology
3. **Integration** — Making AI fit into existing workflows, not replacing them
4. **Education** — Helping teams understand and trust AI capabilities
5. **Process redesign** — Reworking processes to leverage AI, not just adding AI to old processes

The question shifts from "what AI technology do we need?" to "how do we get people to use the technology we have?" ^[inferred]

## The 90% Claim

Linkov's assertion that 90% of technology needed to solve humanity's problems already exists is deliberately provocative. The point isn't the exact number — it's that the gap between available technology and deployed technology is vast, and closing that gap requires organizational effort, not technological breakthroughs. ^[inferred]
