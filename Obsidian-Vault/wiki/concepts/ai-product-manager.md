# AI Product Manager

The **AI product manager** is a role (or more accurately, a mindset) that combines product management judgment with AI and data proficiency to navigate the unique challenges of building AI-powered products. ^[extracted]

## The Traditional Product Management Triangle

Traditional product management sits at the intersection of three areas:

- **Business** — Is the product viable? Is it profitable?
- **Technology** — Is the product feasible? Do we have the right skills?
- **Users** — Is the product desirable? What problem are you solving?

The product manager balances all three to find the right path forward. ^[extracted]

## How AI Changes the Triangle

AI adds complexity to each dimension and introduces a critical new question at the center:

- **Business** — AI products require more experimentation and have higher failure rates. Is the business happy with that?
- **Technology** — How do you evaluate and monitor the performance of AI?
- **Users** — How do you handle the probabilistic nature of AI? What guardrails are needed? How do you build human-in-the-loop?
- **Center** — Is what you're doing even possible? ^[extracted]

## Core Competencies

An AI product manager needs:

1. **Data proficiency** — Understanding the importance of data in AI systems
2. **Evaluation expertise** — Knowing how to build and run evaluations for AI products
3. **Probabilistic thinking** — Comfort with the non-deterministic nature of AI outputs
4. **Human-in-the-loop design** — Knowing where and how to insert human judgment

Both product people and AI engineers can move into this role. For product managers, it means upskilling in AI. For AI engineers, it means leveraging their technical background to move into product management. ^[extracted]

## The Mindset Over Role

James Lowe emphasizes that "AI product manager" is better thought of as a **mindset** than a specific job title. What matters is having someone on the team grappling with all four dimensions (business, technology, users, and AI possibility) to find the path forward. ^[extracted]

This aligns with Brett Taylor's view that there is "a lot of power in combining product and engineering into as few people as possible" and that "few great things have been created by committee." ^[inferred]
