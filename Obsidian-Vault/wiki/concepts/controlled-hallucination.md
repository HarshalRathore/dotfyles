# Controlled Hallucination

Controlled hallucination is the neuroscientific principle that the brain has no direct access to external reality. As [[entities/danielle-perszyk|Danielle Perszyk]] (Amazon AGI) describes it: "Our brains don't have direct access to reality, so they're stuck inside our heads. They can only really do a few things — they can make predictions with their world models, they can take in sensory information, and they can reconcile errors between the two." ^[extracted]

Neuroscientists call the brain a **prediction machine** and view perception as **controlled hallucination**. The key word is *controlled* — the brain's predictions are continuously corrected by sensory input. Perszyk emphasizes: "There's no way I could be standing up here in front of you if I didn't have my hallucinations under control. The controlled part is the critical bit." ^[extracted]

## Hallucinations as Features, Not Bugs

Perszyk argues that hallucinations are not inherently undesirable. They are "necessary because they allow us to go beyond the data." The ability to generate predictions that go beyond available information is what makes intelligence flexible and general. The challenge, for both brains and AI, is not eliminating hallucinations but controlling them. ^[extracted]

> "Hallucinations are necessary because they allow us to go beyond the data. They're features rather than bugs of AI that's flexible like ours. So we just need to figure out how to control them." ^[extracted]

## Shared Hallucination as Communication

Perszyk extends the concept to communication: when two people understand each other, one person's words are influencing the other's hallucinations. "If you're understanding my words, then I'm also influencing your hallucinations." Understanding requires that the listener's brain activates the relevant meanings — including the context-dependent sense of a word. ^[extracted]

## Implication for AI Agents

The controlled hallucination framework suggests that AI reliability is not about eliminating model confabulation entirely, but about building mechanisms (shared environments, models of mind, attention-direction tools) that keep the AI's "hallucinations" aligned with the user's reality and goals. ^[inferred]
